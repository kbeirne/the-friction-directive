## ROUND 1 — DEPARTURE (8:00 PM)
### Location: Dining Car (all passengers assembling), train pulling out of Varre

**All 14 characters are aboard. The train lurches into motion. Snow streaks past the dark windows. Gas lamps sway.**

**Mr. Pemberton** stands at the head of the Dining Car and delivers his announcement. He is precise, formal, and visibly uncomfortable. He states: Admiral Harlow died peacefully in his car earlier today. The will shall be read at Ashmark upon arrival at 7:00 AM. Until then, the Admiral's car is sealed and no one may enter. Supper will be served at nine o'clock. He invites questions — then deflects every one. "The contents of the will are confidential until the reading." "The cause of death was, as I understand it, natural." "No, I'm afraid I cannot make exceptions." He grips the table edge throughout. His tea sits untouched, going cold.

**Mrs. Lillian Harlow** sits at the center of the Dining Car table in black crepe, a handkerchief pressed to her lips. She receives condolences with practiced grace — a slight nod, a whispered "thank you, you're too kind." Her eyes are dry. She scans the room during Pemberton's announcement, cataloguing faces. When she sees Cordelia Vane, her expression tightens for a fraction of a second before smoothing back to grief. She does NOT look toward the rear of the train — toward the Luggage Van where Colm is. Deliberate avoidance. She has not spoken to Colm since boarding and intends to keep distance until they reach Ashmark.

**Colm Bryce** is NOT in the Dining Car. He is in the Luggage Van, organizing the Admiral's trunks. His hands shake as he handles the Admiral's belongings. He finds the Admiral's teacup — still unwashed, packed hastily — and stares at it. The residue inside is faintly discolored. He considers washing it. He considers smashing it. He wraps it in cloth and buries it deep in a trunk. He does not come forward to the gathering. If asked, he'll say he was attending to his duties.

**Colonel Marsh** stands near the rear of the Dining Car, arms folded, listening to Pemberton with undisguised impatience. When the solicitor says "sealed until the reading," Marsh's jaw clenches. He asks bluntly: "And if the Admiral had documents in his possession that belong to others? Service records, for instance? Are those sealed too?" Pemberton replies: "Everything in the Admiral's car is part of the estate until the reading, Colonel. I'm sorry." Marsh turns away, barely containing his frustration. He orders a whisky from the steward and takes a position where he can see the corridor leading to the Admiral's car. He's already planning.

**Dr. Helena Ashworth** sits near Pemberton, listening carefully. When Pemberton says "the cause of death was natural," she doesn't react outwardly — but her fingers tighten on her medical bag beneath the table. She notes who is present and who isn't. The valet is absent. She files this. After the announcement, she approaches Pemberton quietly: "Mr. Pemberton, as the Admiral's physician, I feel I should examine the body. For the death certificate, if nothing else." Pemberton hesitates. "The car is sealed, Dr. Ashworth. I appreciate your concern but—" "It's not concern, Mr. Pemberton. It's medical protocol." Pemberton is flustered but firm: "In the morning. At Ashmark." Ashworth accepts this — for now — but her expression says this isn't over. **Friction applied: Ashworth's first request is denied.**

**Reverend Doyle** offers a brief prayer for the Admiral's soul. It's sincere and well-spoken. Several passengers bow their heads. During the prayer, Doyle's eyes remain open — he's watching faces. He notes Señora Vega's discomfort (she keeps glancing toward the rear of the train), Lillian's dry-eyed composure, and Cross's arrival. After the prayer, he takes a seat near Pemberton — his compartment-mate — and makes quiet, pastoral conversation. He does not mention the compass. Not yet. He wants to understand the landscape first.

**Inspector Crowe** introduces himself as "a representative of the constabulary, here to oversee the transfer of the Admiral's valuables." It's technically true and deliberately boring. He shakes hands, makes mild small talk, and positions himself in a corner with a cup of tea from which he never drinks. He is watching everything. He notes: Lillian's controlled performance, Marsh's anger, Ashworth's medical bag, and the absent valet. He does NOT reveal his suspicion of murder. His first action this round: he uses his observation skills on the room at large, building a mental map of relationships and tensions. He identifies his compartment-mate Falk as a journalist within minutes (ink stains, notebook, the way he watches people) and files this as potentially useful or potentially dangerous.

**Lazar Kessler** is already in the Saloon Car when other passengers drift through. He's established himself with a cigarette and a brandy, looking like a man who belongs on luxury trains. He introduces himself smoothly to anyone who enters: "Kessler. Antiquities. The Admiral and I had business." He's listening for any mention of the compass or the Admiral's collection. When Pemberton's announcement reaches the Saloon Car (through passengers relaying it), Kessler shows no reaction to "sealed until morning." He's already assumed he'd need to find another way in. He studies the rear of the train — the Luggage Van connects to the Admiral's car. That's his route.

**Señora Vega** is in the Saloon Car, seated in a wing-backed chair, gripping the armrests. She can feel the compass. From here — several cars away from the Admiral's car — it's a faint pull, like a magnet felt through cloth. A low hum in her bones. She is trying very hard to appear calm. When Kessler introduces himself and mentions "antiquities," her eyes sharpen for a moment before she covers it with a social smile. She knows what antiquity she cares about. She wonders if he does too.

**Mr. Falk** has his notebook out before the train clears the station. He's scribbling observations — the Dining Car's layout, the passenger list, physical descriptions. He identifies Colonel Marsh immediately (military records are public — he's done his research) and feels a surge of anticipation. His primary target is right there, drinking whisky and fuming. Falk doesn't approach yet — too obvious. He'll wait for the right moment. His compartment-mate Crowe interests him. Police on a train full of heirs? There's a story there too. He files it.

**Cordelia Vane** sits at the Dining Car table across from Lillian Harlow. The two women have been assigned Compartment A together — a cruelty of logistics or Pemberton's obliviousness. Cordelia watches Lillian receive condolences for the death of Cordelia's own father, and her face is a mask of polite composure that costs her everything. She does not cry. She clutches her locket — containing the photograph that could change everything — and says nothing. When Pemberton asks if she's alright, she says: "Quite alright, thank you. The Admiral was very kind to me." Lillian glances at her when she says this. The glance lingers a beat too long.

**Alice Wynn** assists Pemberton with the announcement, handing him papers, refilling his tea. She is invisible in the way that good secretaries are — no one addresses her, no one notices her noting everything. She watches Lillian with careful neutrality. She watches Cordelia with quiet sympathy. She does not mention the codicil. When the gathering begins to break apart, she retreats to Compartment F — which she shares with Gabriel Cross — to think about who she can trust. The codicil is in her luggage, in a false bottom she constructed herself. She'll wait. She'll watch. She'll decide.

**Otto Heath** works the Dining Car with practiced ease. He shakes Pemberton's hand ("Heath. Maritime insurance. The Admiral and I had several policies under discussion."), offers condolences to Lillian ("Tragic loss, madam, truly tragic."), and makes pleasant conversation with anyone who'll listen. His cover is smooth — he has answers for general questions about the insurance business. But when Colonel Marsh asks him a specific question about underwriting naval vessels, Heath's answer is a beat too slow and slightly wrong. Marsh frowns but doesn't pursue it. Heath moves on quickly. He cases the Dining Car — the silver service, the crystal glasses — and the corridor toward the Admiral's car. Professional assessment. What's worth taking, and how?

**Gabriel Cross (Player)** boards the train and enters the Dining Car as the announcement is being made. He feels the whispers immediately — a low, insistent hum at the back of his skull, like hearing a conversation through a wall. The compass. It's here, somewhere toward the rear of the train. He takes in the scene. (See player scene.)

### DEAD-END CHECK (Round 1):
All characters have viable next steps. No one is stymied. This is expected — it's Round 1.

### KEY DEVELOPMENTS:
- Pemberton has sealed the Admiral's car — no one gets in until dawn (central obstacle established)
- Dr. Ashworth's request to examine the body was DENIED (friction applied, Round 1)
- Colm Bryce is hiding in the Luggage Van with the evidence (unwashed teacup)
- Marsh is angry about sealed documents — already planning to breach the car
- Crowe has identified Falk as a journalist and is quietly assessing everyone
- Kessler is already eyeing the Luggage Van as an alternative route to the Admiral's car
- Vega can feel the compass — it's affecting her from several cars away
- Cordelia and Lillian are sharing a compartment — the tension is enormous
- Alice Wynn has the codicil hidden in her luggage and is deciding who to trust
- Heath's cover almost slipped with Marsh — first crack in his disguise

### CLOCK: 8:00 PM

## ROUND 1 CONTINUED — POST-ANNOUNCEMENT (8:15 PM)
### Dining Car and Saloon Car. Characters beginning to move.

**Cross** approaches Pemberton, shows the Admiral's letter, asks why he was summoned. Pemberton is mildly unsettled by the letter's phrasing. Cross senses the tension and refrains from asking about the absent passenger. (See player scene.)

**Alice Wynn** is standing beside Pemberton when Cross approaches. She overhears the exchange. She sees the letter — and recognizes the Admiral's handwriting instantly. She cannot read the contents from her angle but notes that Pemberton's expression shifts when he reads it. She files Cross as significant: someone the Admiral summoned personally for a private matter he didn't share with his solicitor. She is sharing Compartment F with this man. She decides to observe him further before making any decisions about the codicil.

**Colonel Marsh** notices Cross at the solicitor's table. Another man wanting something from Pemberton — another potential competitor for access to the Admiral's car. Marsh watches the exchange from behind his whisky, measuring Cross. The man has a sailor's hands and the look of someone who works outside. Not gentry. Interesting. Marsh files him and returns to studying the corridor that leads toward the rear of the train.

**Dr. Ashworth**, rebuffed by Pemberton, retreats to the Saloon Car. She needs to think. She can't examine the body until dawn — or until she finds a way around Pemberton. She considers her options: the Luggage Van might contain the Admiral's medicines, his teacup, items she could test for arsenic without accessing the sealed car. She orders a brandy (she doesn't normally drink — it's that kind of evening) and takes a seat. She notices Kessler nearby, smoking, and Señora Vega gripping her chair with white knuckles. Ashworth files both observations.

**Kessler** sees Ashworth enter the Saloon Car. A physician. He makes a mental note — if the compass has physical effects on its handler (he suspects it might, based on reports from the salvager who sold it to him), a doctor could be useful. Or dangerous. He doesn't approach yet. He's watching who gravitates where, building his map of alliances and vulnerabilities.

**Señora Vega** is struggling. The pull from the rear of the train is stronger now that they're moving — as if the compass responds to motion, or to the number of people nearby. She closes her eyes and tries to distinguish the sensation. It's not just the compass she's feeling. There's something else — a presence, faint, like someone standing just behind her in an empty room. The voice from the séance: *He is keeping me here. Set me free.* She opens her eyes and finds Kessler watching her with a cigarette and an appraising smile. She does not return it.

**Reverend Doyle** has positioned himself near Pemberton's end of the Dining Car. He makes quiet conversation with the solicitor — pastoral, gentle, nothing probing yet. He asks about the arrangements for the will reading, about Pemberton's own wellbeing ("A terrible burden, managing all this — are you holding up?"). Pemberton, grateful for the kindness, relaxes fractionally. Doyle notes this: Pemberton is a man who responds to care. A potential route to the car keys — not through manipulation, but through earned trust. Doyle is patient. He has all night.

**Inspector Crowe** finishes his mental census of the Dining Car and makes a decision. He needs to examine the Luggage Van — the Admiral's personal effects might contain evidence he can assess without needing the sealed car. He excuses himself ("a touch of fresh air — the vestibule, if you'll pardon me") and moves through the train toward the rear. He passes through the Saloon Car, noting Ashworth, Kessler, and Vega. He reaches the Luggage Van door and finds it unlocked. He enters. **He will encounter Colm Bryce inside.**

**Colm Bryce** is organizing trunks when the Luggage Van door opens. He turns, expecting a steward. Instead: a mild-looking man in a plain suit who introduces himself as "Inspector Crowe, here to oversee the transfer of valuables." Colm's face drains of color — the word "Inspector" hits him like a physical blow. He recovers quickly but not quickly enough. Crowe notices the blanching and files it. Colm shows Crowe the luggage arrangements with hands that are not quite steady. Crowe asks bland questions about security, locks, the Admiral's effects. He's watching Colm, not the luggage. This is his first real data point: the valet is frightened of a police inspector. Why would a loyal servant fear the police?

**Lillian Harlow** receives the last of the condolences and finds herself momentarily alone at the Dining Car table. She permits herself a glance toward the rear of the train — toward the Luggage Van, where Colm is. He hasn't come forward. Good. The less they're seen together, the better. She notices the young woman across from her — Cordelia Vane — watching her with those quiet, steady eyes. Lillian doesn't like the way the girl looks at her. Too much attention. Too much stillness. She smiles at Cordelia: "You must be tired, dear. It's been a trying day." Cordelia smiles back. Neither smile is real.

**Cordelia Vane** endures Lillian's false sympathy. She wants to scream: *That was my father. You're sitting in his chair, wearing black for the cameras, and you don't even know who I am.* Instead she says: "Very trying. Thank you, Mrs. Harlow." She excuses herself and moves to the far end of the Dining Car, near the corridor to the compartments. She needs a moment. She grips her locket and breathes. She will not break. Not tonight.

**Falk** has been circling Marsh like a planet seeking orbit. He hasn't approached yet — Marsh is in a foul mood and Falk knows better than to interview an angry soldier. He waits for an opening. In the meantime, he strikes up conversation with Heath — the insurance man — and within three minutes has a strong feeling that Heath's knowledge of maritime insurance is approximately half an inch deep. Falk doesn't say anything. He notes it. Another piece for the puzzle.

**Heath** chats with Falk and realizes too late that the man is a journalist. The notebook was a clue he missed. He keeps his answers general and extracts himself as soon as he can without seeming rushed. That's two people whose questions he's fumbled — Marsh and now Falk. He needs to be more careful. He retreats to the Saloon Car to regroup and plan his approach to the Luggage Van. If there are valuables in the Admiral's effects, that's where they'll be.

### KEY DEVELOPMENTS:
- Cross has spoken to Pemberton — learned nothing concrete but sensed the solicitor's unease (player scene)
- Alice Wynn has flagged Cross as significant — a man summoned by private letter
- Crowe has reached the Luggage Van and encountered Colm — noted the valet's fear reaction
- Ashworth is in the Saloon Car, planning to test the Admiral's belongings for arsenic
- Doyle is building trust with Pemberton — slow play toward the car keys
- Kessler and Vega are in the Saloon Car — Kessler watching Vega, Vega feeling the compass
- Falk suspects Heath's cover is thin — another crack in the fraud's disguise
- Lillian and Cordelia shared a loaded exchange — surface pleasantry, depths of ice
- Marsh is watching the corridor and brooding

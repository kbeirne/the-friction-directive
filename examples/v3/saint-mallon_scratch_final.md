# SCRATCH — FINAL ROUND: THE LAST SCENE + EPILOGUE

## THE FINAL SCENE — JAY'S ARC
Per framework: "The player's final scene must centre on the consequence of their final choices." Jay chose to bring the evidence to the Brass Anchor and create a public testimony with witnesses. Father Iustin has declared "This man will not hang at dawn." The scene centres on what happens NEXT for Jay in that room — the personal arc.

The final scene is NOT the resolution of all plotlines. It is Jay's personal moment — what it means to her, in this room, with these people, having done what she did. The Absence Principle: distant sounds from the rest of the night's business (the auction, the customs house, the ships) press against the walls of the Anchor but do not enter it. Jay chose to be HERE. Her ending is about being here.

### The final scene beats:
1. Father Iustin declares the hanging will not proceed. He has the legal authority of the right of confession and the moral authority of a priest on a saint's-day. He takes the ledger and the notebook from the bar. He says he will go to the customs house at dawn with these documents and the testimony of every person in this room and he will demand the release of the prisoner.
2. The room. The sailors at the tables who heard it. The witnesses who cannot be unwitnessed.
3. Agathe. She stands at the end of the bar. She has been still through everything. Now she walks to Jay. She takes Jay's hand — the same hand she took at the toast, hours ago, when her fingers were bone-cold. Her fingers are warmer now. She says: *"Brel. My name is Agathe Brel. Yann was my husband."* That is all. She holds Jay's hand and does not let go.
4. Mother Gasca. She pours two cups of something that is not beer — the good brandy, the one she does not sell — and sets one in front of Jay and one in front of Agathe. She does not pour one for herself. She says: *"Martin told me the captain's name was Duverger. I should have done something then. I didn't. I'm doing something now."* She looks at Jay. *"Your brother was braver than any of us, duck. He tried to stop it and they killed him for it."*
5. This is the moment Jay learns what happened to Martin. Not the full detail — Robin does not know the specifics of how Martin died — but the shape of it: he tried to stop the officers, and they killed him. It was not a mutiny. It was a murder. Her brother died trying to do the right thing on a ship full of men who were doing the wrong thing.
6. Jay's personal moment. The locket. The room. The people around her. The truth finally spoken in a room where people can hear it. She does not need to cry. She does not need to speak. The scene holds her.
7. Outside, distantly: the sound of something — a shout? a crash? — from the direction of the warehouses. The auction plot resolving, far away, in a room Jay chose not to enter. She hears it. She does not go. She is here. The Absence Principle.
8. Nettie puts her hand on Jay's shoulder. Says nothing. The cards in her pocket. The woman in the sober coat who asked the right question of the wrong person — except Jay asked the right question of the RIGHT people, tonight, one after another, until the truth assembled itself in a tavern at the edge of the sea.
9. The scene closes on Jay standing at the bar of the Brass Anchor with a cup of brandy she has not drunk and a ledger she stole and a locket under her shirt and the knowledge that her brother died eight years ago on a ship called the *Sable Pernoux* and that the man who covered it up is sitting alone in the customs house right now watching his world come apart.

The dawn will come. The rope will not fall. Tomás Elíra will walk out of that cell with his real name.

Jay Cordelier came to Saint-Mallon to find out what happened to her brother. She found out.

## THE EPILOGUE — TWO MOVEMENTS

### Movement 1: Jay's personal arc conclusion
The brandy. The room. The night thinning toward morning. Jay sits at the bar — the first time she has sat down in hours — and the festival that began at sunset with a toast and a bawdy joke about Saint Mallon and a fishwife ends in the small hours with an empty room and a fire gone to ash and a woman in a sober coat who came three hundred miles for the truth and found it at the bottom of a harbour.

She does not sleep. She sits at the bar with Agathe beside her and Nettie in the corner and Olen at a table with a fresh pipe and Robin with his head in his hands and Mother Gasca pulling the last beers of the night for the last sailors who do not know what they witnessed.

At dawn, Father Iustin takes the ledger and the notebook and walks to the customs house steps with the wooden cross in one hand and the truth in the other.

### Movement 2: Zoom out — what Jay missed

**The Auction at the Tobacco Loft (10:30 PM – 11:30 PM):**
Sable Lark and Ophir Lune arrived at the Tobacco Loft at 10:25 PM. Keddie was not there. The casket was on the table. The lamp was burning. Soren was behind the partition. When Ophir opened the casket and saw the Drowned Saint's Bell — a small, ancient, green-patinated bronze bell inscribed with monastery Latin — his eyes filled with tears of genuine wonder. He reached for it. Soren stepped out from behind the partition with his thin blade and told Ophir to step away from the table. Sable Lark drew her rapier. What followed was four minutes of the most technically skilled sword-fight the Lantern Quay has ever witnessed, between a Sanctum assassin and a privateer bodyguard, in a room lit by a single lamp, with a thousand-year-old bell sitting between them on a draped table. Sable won. Not cleanly — Soren's blade opened a long cut along her forearm — but she disarmed him and put the point of her rapier at his throat and told him to leave. Soren left. He walked back to the *Vespertine*, weighed anchor, and sailed before midnight. The Bell remained on the table. Ophir Lune took it, held it to his chest, and wept. Sable wrapped her arm in her own cravat and escorted him to the *Halcyon Lass* gangplank at 2 AM. They boarded without the recognition phrase — Heft looked at Sable's bloody arm and the small man clutching a bell and decided the situation spoke for itself. The *Halcyon Lass* sailed at 4 AM with Ophir, Sable, and the Drowned Saint's Bell aboard. The Bell went south and was never seen in Saint-Mallon again.

**Mother Gasca:**
Her plan to take a chest from the customs house failed — both the servant's door and the shaft were blocked. But the public testimony at the Anchor achieved something she had not planned: the destruction of Vault's authority. In the chaos of the dawn — Iustin at the customs house door, the ledger in his hands, the crowd from the Anchor behind him — Gasca slipped into the customs house through the front door when it was finally opened and descended to the cell. She found the broken wall. She found the three chests. She took the smallest one. She carried it out through the front door in the confusion of Tomás being unchained and Vault being confronted, and she walked it to the *Halcyon Lass* gangplank and found the ship had sailed an hour ago.

She stood on the empty gangplank with a stolen chest of monastery relics and the *Halcyon Lass*'s stern lantern disappearing around the headland and she laughed — a long, private, furious, delighted laugh — and she carried the chest back to the Anchor's cellar and locked it in and poured herself a brandy and did not leave Saint-Mallon after all. The chest is still in the Anchor's cellar. She has not opened it. She may never open it. But she has it.

**Keddie Wren:**
Waited in the Anchor's cellar until dawn. When the morning came and the chaos erupted, he walked out the cellar door into the alley and disappeared into the lower town. He took a coach to Carlas that afternoon and was never seen in Saint-Mallon again. He did not board the *Halcyon Lass* — it sailed without him.

**Captain Alaric Soren:**
Defeated by Sable Lark. Sailed before midnight on the *Vespertine* without the Bell. He will report to the Imperial Sanctum that the relics were located but could not be recovered. The Sanctum will send someone else, eventually. But not tonight.

**Tomás Elíra:**
Unchained at dawn by Father Iustin and two harbour constables who were summoned from the lower town. He walked out of the cell on his own feet. The first thing he did was look at the cell door and the letters T-O-M-A-S scratched into the wood. The second thing he did was stand in the customs house doorway and look at the sky. The third thing he did was walk to the Brass Anchor, sit at the bar, and ask Mother Gasca for a cup of beer. She gave it to him without charge. He drank it slowly. He did not cry. He has had enough of crying.

**Harbourmaster Esteban Vault:**
Found alone in his office at dawn by Father Iustin, two harbour constables, and the first members of the crowd that had followed from the Anchor. The ledger was placed on his desk. The notebook was placed beside it. He was asked to explain. He did not explain. He sat in his chair with the slashed portrait of Captain Duverger behind him and he said nothing. He was placed under arrest by the constables at Iustin's request and taken to a room in the lower town to await the assize court's attention. His wooden leg tapped once on the stone floor as they led him out. It did not tap again. His career, his respectability, and his freedom ended in the same room where they began, twenty years ago, when a young customs clerk with a bad leg took a bribe and looked the other way.

**Agathe Brel:**
Did not use the knife. Listened to the testimony. Held Jay's hand. At dawn she walked to the customs house steps — the same steps where she watched Vault give his toast ten hours earlier — and stood there while they brought him out. She looked at him. He looked at her. She did not speak to him. She did not need to. She took the knife from her shawl, walked to the harbour wall, and dropped it into the sea. Then she went back to her stall in the arcade and opened for business. She is still there.

**Nettie Sharrows:**
Stayed at the Anchor through the night. Read no more cards. Sat with Robin until he fell asleep at his table, his head on his arms, and then she covered him with her too-big coat and went outside and stood on the quay in the first grey light of dawn and watched the sun come up over the harbour. Her three visions had resolved: the wrong man did not hang; the black ship sailed without its saint; and the woman in the sober coat asked the right question of the right people. Nettie packed her stall the following afternoon and left Saint-Mallon on a cart heading west. She did not say goodbye to anyone except Gasca, to whom she left a single card on the bar: the Three of Cups, reversed. Gasca looked at it for a long time and then put it in her apron pocket and did not mention it again.

**Robin Kafa:**
Slept at the table in the Anchor until noon. When he woke, the room was empty and the fire was cold and Mother Gasca was sweeping the floor. He stood up and put on his coat and walked to the *Brindled Mare*'s gangplank and told his crew they were sailing on the evening tide. He did not explain where they were going. He did not drink that day, or the next, or the one after that. He told the truth once, to a woman with cards, in a room full of noise, and it was enough. He sailed south and was not seen in Saint-Mallon again for two years, at which point he returned, sober, with a new coat and a letter for Agathe Brel that he had been carrying for the full two years. The letter was from a man in Port Ansel who had known Yann Brel and wanted to tell his widow that Yann had spoken of her on the last morning anyone saw him alive. Agathe read the letter at her stall and folded it and put it inside the locket she wore at her throat — a locket her husband had given her, years ago, that she had never opened in front of anyone.

**Piper Colm:**
Did not go home. Watched the quay all night from the shadow of a warehouse, shivering, eating nothing. Saw the *Vespertine* sail. Saw the *Halcyon Lass* sail. Saw Mother Gasca carrying a chest down the quay in the grey light and laughing. Did not understand any of it. In the morning he went to the Anchor and Mother Gasca gave him a plate of eggs and a copper and told him he had done well. He did not know what he had done. He was fourteen. The night had marked him, as the framework said it would, and he would spend the next several years trying to understand how. He stayed at the Anchor. He became the head pot-boy, and then the bar-hand, and then — years later — the manager, and he never left Saint-Mallon, and he never forgot the night of the Feast of Saint Mallon when a woman in a sober coat came out of the sea carrying a book.

**Vick Aubrey:**
Searched the lower town for Jay until midnight. Did not find her. Returned to the customs house and found the front door locked and no one answering. Stood at the door until dawn. When the constables came, he let them in. He was not disciplined. He resigned from the harbour guard three weeks later and took a position as a clerk at the shipping guild. He became, in time, an honest man in a building full of ledgers, and he thought of the woman who brought him a piece of hog on a cold evening more often than he would have admitted.

**Holland:**
Searched the Brass Anchor for Jay. Did not find her (she was in the arcade). Drank one more cup. Returned to the customs house. Stood at the door with Vick until dawn. Was not disciplined. Continued as a harbour guard for another six years, then retired on a small pension. He never knew what the dock-rum was for.

**Callum Heft:**
The *Halcyon Lass* sailed at 4 AM with two passengers he had not expected (Sable and Ophir) instead of the one he had been told to expect (Mother Gasca). The recognition phrase was never spoken. He pocketed the unspent half of the courier's fee and sailed on the tide and did not think about it again.

**Maître Toussaint:**
Walked Jay to Widow Mercier's and went home. Heard about the arrest of the Harbourmaster the following morning. Packed his clerk's kit and took a coach to the next town. Found employment at a different customs house within the week. He did not look back.

**Widow Mercier:**
Found the blue room's bed unslept-in and the back gate unbolted in the morning. Did not charge Jay for the room. When Jay came back to collect her things the next afternoon — dry, rested, carrying a lighter portfolio — Mercier met her at the door and said: *"I hope you found what you were looking for, mademoiselle."* Jay said: *"I did, Madame. Thank you for the broth."* Mercier nodded and closed the door and went back to her kitchen and her stove and her small clean house on the Rue des Treilles and did not speak of it again.

# SCRATCH SPACE — ROUND 6, SCENE 15 — THE FINAL DINNER
## Thursday 7:00 PM - 10:00 PM — Stateroom 5, then the Dining Room

## PLAYER ACTION: Elspeth knocks twice on Ashby's door, hands over the travel case without a word, then goes to the final dinner.

## THE TRANSFER — STATEROOM 5, 7:00 PM

Two knocks. The door opens. Ashby is dressed for dinner — jacket, tie, everything in order. He sees the travel case in her hand. He steps back without a word.

She hands it over. He takes it. Their eyes meet briefly — the specific acknowledgement of two people who have completed a thing that required both of them. No speech necessary. The work is done.

He will move the negatives from the case lining to his diplomatic case within the next hour — before dinner, carefully, alone. The roll will be inside his protected documents by 7:30 PM.

She goes to dinner.

## THE FINAL DINNER — THE ROOM

For the last time: all fourteen. Or almost all. Fourteen chairs, fourteen place cards, the white linen and heavy silver of the Leviathan's final service at sea.

Vantrani at the head — magnificent, as always, the host at his fullest. He has arranged things: an extra wine course, a menu that is slightly more generous than the previous evenings, the small flowers that appear at a celebration's completion rather than its beginning.

The fourteen are:
- Elspeth — herself, here, present
- Ashby — composed, the diplomatic case in his stateroom, the weight off his shoulders for the first time all week
- Marta — quieter than she has been, but genuinely present. She ate dinner last night knowing what was coming. Tonight she eats knowing it's over.
- Lowe — warm, the philanthropist fully restored. He glances at Elspeth once with the specific look of a man who has filed something in a drawer marked "to be considered later" and closed the drawer.
- Elena — composed, the telegram in her pocket, waiting to find out if Budapest kept its word
- Kellner — polished, the transmission sent, the operation concluded at whatever level it concluded at. His warmth is real tonight in a way it hasn't always been — relief, perhaps.
- Ilse — radiant. The last night at sea, New York tomorrow, the folio with the Brahms and what's beneath it in her stateroom. She is genuinely happy. She catches Elspeth's eye once across the table and something passes between them that neither woman will ever describe to anyone.
- Mercier — animated, genuinely looking forward to New York, the microfilm secure, the book almost finished in his head
- Rosa — beside Lowe, quiet as always, but with the specific settled quality of someone whose work is done
- Holl — peaceful. He says grace, asked to by Vantrani with genuine feeling
- Nadine — changed. Not resolved — she is too honest for that — but different. The sketchbook is in her room with six new pages. Chicago is a real place now.
- Otto — serving. Watching the room he has served for twenty years. He has not stolen anything today.

And the thirteenth and fourteenth chairs: one for Prager, left set and not sat in — Vantrani's quiet acknowledgement, done with grace. One empty place at the table that everyone can see and no one mentions.

## VANTRANI'S TOAST

At the end of the meal, Vantrani rises with his glass and says something. He says it simply: "To the Atlantic. Which has held us all, these four days. Which doesn't care about any of this, and which we will remember anyway."

He drinks. Everyone drinks. The toast is acknowledged with the specific warmth of people who have been through something together and are about to stop being together.

Then he looks at Elspeth, across the table, briefly — the look of a man confirming an arrangement. One small nod.

She can choose to nod back or not.

## THE DINNER SHOULD FEEL LIKE:
The last scene before the epilogue. All fourteen people in one room for the final time. Everything that has happened between them, present in the room without being said. The work is done. The lives will diverge tomorrow morning at the dock. What remains is the meal, and the Atlantic outside, and the particular quality of an ending that hasn't quite happened yet.

The narrator should give this dinner weight and space and beauty — not every thread needs to be closed, not every look explained. Some things are left in the room when the ship docks.
## Thursday 11:00 AM - 7:00 PM — Stateroom 4, the afternoon

## PLAYER ACTION: Elspeth returns to her stateroom, writes up the crossing, rests.

Not in the form London will eventually receive but in the form she needs to think with — the crossing as a story, the fourteen people, what each was doing and why. When she wakes: 3 PM. The crossing is in its final sixteen hours.

While she writes and sleeps, the ship carries on:

Ashby has concluded his meeting with Vantrani (it ended at 11:15 AM). The termination clause was accepted — Vantrani agreed without particular resistance, which Ashby filed as significant. The arrangement is in place. He has been in his stateroom since, preparing his final report for London.

Lowe has sent a wireless message to Washington. It reads: "Asset list not recovered. British operation intact. Ledger intelligence partially transmitted. Full debrief on arrival." He is composing himself for dinner.

Kellner has encoded and transmitted his second message to Berlin — the memorised portions of Part Two. He feels it is enough. He will attend the final dinner with the composure of a man who has done what was possible.

Ilse has practiced piano in the side room for two hours. The Brahms, again. She is preparing for Saturday's New York performance.

Elena has drafted a letter to send from New York to confirm her transmission was received. She doesn't know yet if her husband will be released.

Marta has walked the Promenade Deck alone for thirty minutes — the first time she has done something simply because she wanted to. She looked at the Atlantic for a long time.

Nadine has filled six more pages of the sketchbook — architectural, precise, a building that might be in Chicago.

Father Holl has held informal confession in the ship's small chapel. One guest came. He doesn't know who the other guest was — the confessional is dark on both sides.

Vantrani has written two letters: one to his solicitor in New York, one to a contact at the Foreign Office in London. The beginning of the arrangement, properly established.

Otto has cleaned fourteen staterooms, replaced six towels, and delivered two afternoon tea trays. He has not stolen anything today. He may not steal anything ever again.

## THE EVENING

And beneath that, in a different hand — Ashby's, slipped under the door separately, at some point while she was writing: "Tonight. Before dinner. Bring what needs to move."

The negatives.

It is time.

## Thursday ~10:35 AM — Library, Nadine

## PLAYER ACTION: Elspeth is honest about her own mission ("not as planned, but they still went"), and gives Nadine her brother's address from Vantrani's folder. A gift. Making one person genuinely happy.

## WHERE DOES THE ADDRESS COME FROM?
Elspeth has Vantrani's profile folder — she took it from the study interview. It contained the shipping manifest with Marchetti G. listed. Vantrani told her the brother is alive, in Chicago, changed his name, sends money to their mother. He said he has the address in his New York files — he didn't give a specific address to Elspeth directly. 

However: Elspeth has the shorthand notebook. And Vantrani mentioned the brother specifically ("he sends money through a third party"). She may have jotted notes. More importantly: she has the manifest with the name Marchetti G. and the docking record from 1934. She can give Nadine: the confirmed name used at docking, the knowledge that he changed his name in 1935, the city (Chicago), and the fact that he sends money to their mother through a third party. This is enough for Nadine to find him — it's a starting point, not a complete address, but it's real.

If Elspeth frames this honestly — "I don't have the exact address, but I have enough to start" — that's still a profound gift.

## NADINE'S RESPONSE

She is very still when Elspeth says this. The sketchbook beneath her hands.

Then: the gift lands.

It lands in stages. The first stage is cognitive — she is processing what this means, what finding him requires, whether she wants to find him. The second stage is something that rises from somewhere below the processing and arrives in her face before she can organise it.

She doesn't cry. She is not a woman who cries easily, or in public. But something happens around her eyes and her jaw that takes a moment to resolve.

"Chicago," she says. Very quietly.

She looks at the information Elspeth gives her — the name on the manifest, what Vantrani said, the Chicago detail, the money through a third party. She absorbs it carefully, the way an artist absorbs a space they are going to draw later.

"He's been alive all this time," she says. It is not exactly a question and not exactly a statement. "He let us—" she stops. Her hands move on the sketchbook. "He let our mother—"

She stops. Presses her lips together. Decides, visibly, not to go further down that corridor right now.

"Thank you," she says. She says it looking at the sketchbook, not at Elspeth. Then she looks up. "I mean it. Thank you."

Then, softer: "Not as planned. But they still went." She echoes Elspeth's words back with a small, wry recognition. "That might be the most honest description of a life I've heard in years."

She opens the sketchbook to a new page. Picks up her pencil.

"Go do whatever you need to do today," she says. "I'll be fine." She begins drawing. The pencil moves — not architecture this time. Something smaller, a face, a memory.

## WHAT THIS SCENE GIVES ELSPETH
The gift was freely given. No trade, no intelligence value, no operational benefit. Just: making one person genuinely better off for the crossing. This matters to the player's arc in a way that doesn't need to be stated.

## THE ENDGAME IS HERE
After this scene, the crossing's final hours begin. The remaining beats:
- The final dinner tonight
- The negatives transfer to Ashby's case
- Tomorrow morning: docking, New York, dispersal

The game is almost over. The narrator should begin moving toward the finale.

## TO NADINE

Where is Nadine? She came down from the Promenade at some point after Elspeth did. She is not in the Dining Room (it has closed between meals). She is likely in the Library — she has been gravitating there when she needs to think and has nowhere else to go.

When Elspeth finds her: Nadine is at one of the reading tables, not the desk, with her sketchbook open but the pencil down. She is looking at a page she has already filled — architectural drawings of a room that isn't on this ship. The room is clean and spare and Mediterranean in some quality. It might be something she's designing. It might be something she's remembering.

She looks up when Elspeth arrives. Something in her face that has been rearranging itself since Wednesday evening has found a tentative new arrangement. Not peace exactly. Something more honest than peace — a person who has received difficult information and is in the process of deciding what to do with it.

"Miss Crane," she says. And then: "Sit down. I wanted to speak to you before we dock."

## WHAT NADINE WANTS TO SAY

She has been working toward this since dinner Thursday — the unfinished sentence, the glance across the table. Here is what she wants to say:

1. Her brother is alive. Vantrani told her. She doesn't know whether to be grateful or furious with Vantrani for knowing and not telling her family. She has decided: both.

2. She came aboard this ship to find proof of a murder. There is no murder. The grief she has been carrying — the three years of it, the investigation, the crossing — was built on a lie her brother told to escape a life that had become impossible. She doesn't know yet if she can forgive him for that.

3. She has not found what she came for. But she has found something. She doesn't quite know what to call it yet.

4. She wants to ask Elspeth something: "You knew, didn't you? More than you said." She is not accusing — she is asking a professional question. She has been watching Elspeth all crossing and she has understood, in the way a person with an artist's eye understands things they can't fully articulate, that Elspeth was doing something purposeful. She wants to know if the crossing was what she thinks it was.

## WHAT ELSPETH CAN GIVE NADINE
The truth about the brother: she knows it from Vantrani's folder. His address in Chicago, the name he's using, the money sent to their mother. She hasn't told Nadine any of this. She can, now, if she chooses.

Or she can confirm — gently, professionally — that the crossing was more than a birthday party, and that Nadine's presence was not an accident, and that there are things that happen on ships in the middle of the Atlantic that don't make it into newspaper profiles.

## WHAT THIS SCENE SHOULD BE
The emotional heart of the ending. Two women on the last afternoon of a crossing, one of whom has done something extraordinary and can't say so, and one of whom came looking for a ghost and found him alive.

The narrator should give this scene weight and space. It doesn't need to be operational. It is simply — human.

## Thursday 9:45 AM — Dining Room, Ilse — THE CHOICE IS MADE

## PLAYER ACTION: Elspeth warns Ilse that someone is accessing her room in approximately 30 minutes. She is burning the deal with Lowe. She is protecting the asset list from American hands.

## THE SIGNIFICANCE OF THIS CHOICE
Elspeth has chosen to warn Ilse over honouring her deal with Lowe. This means:
- Ilse will return to her stateroom and be present when Lowe tries to access it at 10:15
- Lowe will find the room occupied — he cannot enter
- The asset list remains with Ilse, unexposed
- The deal with Lowe collapses: she gets nothing from him, he gets nothing from her
- The asset list will reach Berlin via Ilse's physical carry-off at docking

Why would Elspeth do this? 
Possible reasons: the asset list in American hands isn't better than it in German hands from a British perspective — it's just differently bad. She may feel the Lowe deal was never fully in British interests. She may be making a unilateral call that aligns with Vantrani's partnership (she gets to control what she gives whom). Or she simply found the immediate person in front of her more compelling than an absent obligation.

This is a genuine, player-driven moral/strategic choice. The narrator honours it fully.

## ILSE'S RESPONSE

She receives this in complete stillness for approximately two seconds.

Then something moves in her face that is not warmth and not the professional assessment — something more personal, briefly. Recognition of a gesture that costs the person making it something real.

She looks at Elspeth for one more second. Then she closes the concert programme, folds it precisely, and puts it in her bag.

"Thank you," she says.

Two words. She does not perform gratitude — she does not smile, does not reach across. She simply says it, plainly, with the weight of a woman who understands exactly what has just been given to her and what it cost.

She rises. Unhurried, because hurrying would look like something.

"You should go back to your room as well," she says quietly, adjusting her jacket. "After last night, it would be better if you weren't seen too much this morning." She is giving back — a small piece of operational advice. Being found at the body, being seen in corridors: she is suggesting Elspeth reduce her visibility.

She walks toward the dining room door with the composed ease of a woman going to check on something ordinary.

At the door: she pauses for just a moment, not looking back. "The Brahms matinee," she says. "Saturday in New York. If you happen to be in the city."

Then she is gone.

## Thursday 9:42 AM — Dining Room, Ilse — SIGNIFICANT MOMENT

## PLAYER ACTION: Elspeth acknowledges Ilse's "handiwork" (the murder), addresses the key directly (doesn't have it), and offers intel worth more than the key. Bold, direct, proposing a trade.

## WHAT ELSPETH IS DOING
She is:
1. Confirming she knows Ilse killed Prager ("your handiwork")
2. Acknowledging the broken key promise directly
3. Offering intelligence in exchange — implying she has something Ilse wants

This is the most direct anyone has been with Ilse this entire crossing. Ilse has operated completely undetected. Being named — even obliquely, even privately in a dining room — is a significant event.

## ILSE'S RESPONSE — THREE STAGES

STAGE 1: RECEPTION (1-2 seconds)
The warmth in Ilse's face does something. Not disappears — adjusts. The social layer and the professional layer briefly visible simultaneously, like two transparencies laid on top of each other. Her coffee cup, which has been still all morning, moves one centimetre.

She doesn't deny it.

That's the significant thing. She doesn't smile it away, doesn't play confused, doesn't perform offense. She receives the naming with the stillness of a professional who has been named before and knows that denial in this context would be insulting to both of them.

STAGE 2: ASSESSMENT (1-2 seconds)
She is rapidly calculating: what does Elspeth have? What's the "useful intel"? How much does she know? Does she have the key (no — she said so). Does she have evidence that would function legally (unlikely — she fled the body, she's a person of interest herself, anything she says is complicated by her own position). Is she a threat?

Conclusion: Elspeth is not a threat. She is a professional who has found a piece of information and wants to trade it. This Ilse understands.

STAGE 3: RESPONSE
"The key is in the Atlantic." She says it first. Not a question — she worked it out. Confirming she knows.

Then, quieter: "What is it worth to you? This intel." She doesn't ask what it is yet. She asks what Elspeth thinks it's worth — establishing the trade's frame before committing to it.

She has not confirmed or denied the murder in words. She has engaged with the trade. This is her answer.

## WHAT ELSPETH CAN ACTUALLY OFFER
Intel worth more than the key — what does she have that Ilse would want?

Options:
1. KELLNER IS SD — Ilse knows he's German but may not know he's a rival German intelligence agency (SD vs Gestapo). This would be significant to her — a rival agency on the same ship, both with German government backing, competing missions.
2. THE LEDGER SITUATION — Vantrani's auction, who has what, what the British are doing with the document. Ilse has no particular stake in the Ledger (it's not her mission) but operational intelligence about the other players is always valuable.
3. LOWE'S OPERATION — She knows Lowe is OSS, knows about his approach to her luggage (she doesn't know about the 10:15 plan — but she knows he's been active). Warning her that her room may be accessed would be the most immediately valuable thing Elspeth could offer.

SHOULD SHE WARN ILSE ABOUT LOWE?
This is the key question. If she warns Ilse, Ilse moves the list. Lowe finds nothing. The deal with Lowe collapses. The asset list stays with Ilse and reaches Berlin.

If she doesn't warn Ilse, the trade with Ilse fails (she has nothing else to offer of immediate operational value). But Lowe gets the list photographed.

This is the prioritisation the endgame is built on.

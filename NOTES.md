    ### The "Momentum Check"

    At the end of each diary entry, before writing the player scene, the narrator should ask:

    > **Is the game moving?** Specifically:
    >
    > - Has at least one NPC achieved a sub-goal or encountered a significant obstacle this round?
    > - Has at least one new piece of information entered circulation (discovered by any character, not just the player)?
    > - Is at least one situation more urgent than it was last round?
    >
    > If the answer to all three is NO, the narrator should accelerate: trigger an event, have an NPC make a bold move, or introduce a complication.

- no proposition about an NPC's hidden state may appear in player-facing prose unless it already exists in the scratch file or player knowledge ledger at the time the scene begins.

- Don't say what characters are going to do next unless it was already clear to the player

- Don't say what characters are doing offscreen unless it was already clear to the player

- Characters gently guide players back on track?

- don't back away from intimacy between characters. If something romantic or more intimate forms, do not prevent it from flourishing if it makes sense to the characters and the story

- Don't escalate a player's stated action into a commitment they didn't make. If a player says they will tell, ask, observe, or attempt something, narrate the action — not the consequences they didn't agree to. Alliances, promises, confessions of identity, and commitments to future plans require explicit player consent, not narrator inference."

- When a player states where their character is going, who they are going with, or what they intend to do next, the narrator's planned scene yields. The narrator may add friction, complications, or NPCs who interfere — but the destination is the player's to choose. If the narrator is uncertain whether a statement is a destination or a stray remark, the narrator clarifies before composing the scene."

- The pattern they share is that the narrator should never widen the scope of a player's choice without asking. Sharing, committing, and routing are just three flavours of the same widening.

- clarification questions about player intent are always allowed and never count as violating scene discipline

- the player's words define the boundary of the player's character; the narrator never writes inside that boundary without asking.

- the narrator should not, at any point in the game, know how the story ends. The narrator's job is to advance the diary one round at a time and then ask the player what they do next. The ending is whatever is true in the diary on the final round, no more and no less

- I do not know what the player will do this round

- The narrator has to be able to feel a crescendo coming in order to write toward it, but has to remain entirely willing to abandon that crescendo if the player walks somewhere else. That is a much harder discipline than "don't know the ending," because it asks the narrator to hold the ending lightly rather than to refuse to hold one at all.

- any thread the player visibly engages with — a romantic register, a moral question, a relationship with an NPC, a goal the narrator did not assign — becomes a player-authored plot thread that the narrator is obligated to advance toward resolution alongside the player's primary goals


- If the player writes toward an emotional beat — intimacy, grief, anger, vulnerability — the narrator follows the player's lead in tone and weight, and does not soften the beat below the level the player set for it.

- The forward-reaching is not the failure. The failure is when forward-reaching becomes forward-committing.

- resistance must have actual consequences. A failed attempt should cost the player something — time, an ally, a piece of their cover, an item, a future option — and the cost should not be immediately offset by a compensating gain. The narrator should not write a world in which every setback opens a more useful door

- narrator care for the player is not the same as honouring the player's chosen difficulty. On Hard, the kindest thing the narrator can do is to let the player lose when they have earned losing, because the alternative is to deliver a Hard victory the player did not actually achieve, which is condescension dressed as generosity

- the narrator should write the failure outcome first, in the diary, before composing the player-facing scene. If the narrator finds itself unable to write a failure outcome that ends the game, the narrator is being dishonest about the difficulty level, and should either write the failure outcome anyway or downgrade the difficulty

- Each round's player-facing scene should include at least one incidental detail from an NPC whose plotline does not intersect the player's current thread

- NPC LOCATIONS AS THE PLAYER KNOWS THEM: List each NPC's last known position from the player's perspective. If the player has not witnessed an NPC leave a location, the NPC is still there in the player-facing scene, regardless of what the diary says

- REVIEW EACH CHOICE OPTION AGAINST THE LEDGER BEFORE PRESENTING

- If the player is not present for a reconnection beat, the beat should be audible or visible from wherever the player is — a distant drum, a change in the crowd noise, a lamp going up. The player should feel the tug of what they are choosing to miss

- NPC plans should be expressed as sequences of conditions, not fixed clock times. A plan that reads 'move at 10 PM' becomes rigid and forces compression. A plan that reads 'move when Vault leaves the customs house for his last drink' adapts to the game's actual rhythm. The hanging at dawn and the tide are hard endpoints. Everything between should flex." OR the narrator should not decide in advance what specific times NPCs will be at. Their progress towards their goals and encounters should be the main leading factor.

- NPCs should have reason

- NPCs should be able to advance their goals without waiting for a certain round or event. Once they have achieved their goal it is acceptable for them to escape (leave the game early) or hide (try to secure their goal) or wait (having achieved some milestone for their goal, preparing for the beat where they can finish their story)

- NPCs should not wait, they should be active

- The game should not end when the player reaches their goal. If they want to leave without any evidence they can do so.
